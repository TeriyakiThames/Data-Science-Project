Abstract:

This contribution is concerned with the development of efficient aperture sampling algorithms for down looking SAR applications. Therefore, we propose a solution that is based on a randomized aperture sampling approach to minimize the number of antenna positions. Since we aim to accelerate the scanning process of the synthetic aperture, the main task arising is to minimize the distance travelled by the sensor. To solve this, a solution is presented that is based on the nearest neighbor algorithm. Finally, the influence of the randomized sampling will be investigated by means of simulated datasets as well as true measurements.

Contents

I. Introduction

In the past years, the development of radar sensors in the terahertz (THz) regime has made great progress. Therefore, radar sensors with frequencies above 100 GHz are state of the art, for instance the frequency modulated continuous wave (FMCW) radar used in this publication ranges from 126 GHz up to 182 GHz [1]. The great benefit of high carrier frequencies comes with an increased bandwidth and as a result, a much more accurate range resolution [2]. For this reason, THz sensors are extremely precise, opening new fields of applications like vibrometry [3]. However, imaging systems based on synthetic aperture radar (SAR) usually require an antenna spacing of at least the quarter of a wavelength. Therefore, an increase in the frequency also scales the amount of data required for the imaging process. Several approaches have been published in the past, on how to deal with this increased amount of necessary measurements. Commonly used methods are approaches from the field of compressed sensing (CS), like in [4]. Here, a special system design is required, that allows to reconstruct the missing signals by applying -optimization. Besides those approaches, we already demonstrated the option of randomly thinning a fully sampled synthetic aperture in [5]. Here, it turned out that a random sampling of the aperture leads to an increased noise floor but generally, avoids systematic errors due to sub-Nyquist sampling of the cross-range chirp signal. However, the previous contribution did not aim to minimize the required time for the measurement process, since the motion of the sensor kept unaffected. Therefore, this paper provides a simple approach for planning a sufficiently short trajectory and as a result a decreased measurement time.
